Georgia is undergoing a significant reshuffle of its top law enforcement leadership, with Prime Minister Irakli Kobakhidze announcing major structural and personnel changes across the State Security Service of Georgia (SSSG), the Ministry of Internal Affairs, and the Adjara Government.

## New State Minister Post Created

The centrepiece of the announcement is the creation of a new, yet-to-be-legislated position: **State Minister for Coordination of Law Enforcement Bodies**. This represents a structural expansion of the executive branch's coordination authority over Georgia's security services.

Current State Security Service Chief **Mamuka Mdinaradze** is set to assume this new role, returning to political activity from what Kobakhidze described as a period of stepping away from political duties. Upon assuming the new position, Mdinaradze will also take on the title of Vice Prime Minister — signalling the elevated weight the government is placing on this coordination mandate.

## Three-Way Leadership Rotation

The changes involve a sequential leadership rotation across three key posts:

- **Geka Geladze**, currently Interior Minister, will succeed Mdinaradze as head of the State Security Service of Georgia
- **Sulkhan Tamazashvili**, until now Chair of the Adjara Autonomous Republic Government (a post he has held since April 2025), will succeed Geladze as Interior Minister
- **Maka Botchorishvili**, Georgia's Foreign Minister, is also appointed Vice Prime Minister alongside Mdinaradze

Kobakhidze indicated that a new chair for the Adjara Government will be named "in the coming days."

## Context: Reform Narrative

Speaking on the changes, Kobakhidze praised Mdinaradze's tenure as SSSG chief as "very successful," citing "systemic reforms" within the agency and "particularly successful" anti-corruption work. Geladze and Tamazashvili were likewise described as having performed "very successfully" in their current roles.

The reshuffle arrives amid ongoing scrutiny of Georgia's law enforcement institutions from international bodies. Council of Europe Commissioner for Human Rights Michael O'Flaherty, wrapping up a visit to Georgia in April 2026, reiterated calls for **law enforcement accountability** — specifically for violence used against protesters in 2024–2025 — and welcomed an Interior Ministry legislative proposal requiring all law enforcement personnel to wear visible identification numbers.

The new coordination minister role may be read as an attempt to centralise oversight of exactly these reformed and accountable law enforcement functions under a single political authority.

## What This Means for Residents and Businesses

Structural changes at the top of Georgia's law enforcement hierarchy can signal shifts in operational priorities — from anti-corruption enforcement intensity to internal security posture — that may indirectly affect:

- **Corporate compliance and regulatory risk**: Leadership changes at SSSG can affect the focus and pace of economic crime investigations
- **Visa and residency processing**: The Interior Ministry oversees immigration and residency permit operations; a new minister may mean procedural adjustments
- **Administrative appeal pathways**: Changes in law enforcement leadership can sometimes affect the independence or workload of affiliated investigative and appeal bodies
