# Daily Crypto Intelligence Routine
## A Practical Guide for the Active Crypto Job Hunter

---

## Philosophy

Crypto moves at the speed of community energy. The protocols that win are the ones where talented builders are actively engaged, shipping, and shaping direction. Your job search isn't just about applying to listings—it's about demonstrating that you're already embedded in the fabric of the ecosystem. This guide gives you a structured, sustainable routine for doing exactly that.

**Core principle:** Spend 30-60 minutes per day on information consumption, 2-4 hours per week on active contribution, and protect your mental energy like it's a scarce resource (because it is).

## 2. Weekly Activities (2-4 Hours/Week)

### Saturday or Sunday: Deep Dive Session (60-90 min)

Pick one topic per week and go deep. Rotate through:

1. **Protocol Architecture Review** — Read the smart contract code of one protocol. GMX v2 contracts on GitHub are well-documented. Understand *why* they made certain design choices.
2. **Governance Proposal Review** — Find an active AIP (Aave Improvement Proposal) or GMC (GMX Governance) proposal. Understand the problem, the proposed solution, the tradeoffs.
3. **Technical Tutorial Run-Through** — Build something small using a protocol's documentation. This is both learning and potential portfolio material.

**Suggested rotation:**

| Week | Focus |
|------|-------|
| Week 1 | Protocol X architecture + write summary |
| Week 2 | Governance proposal analysis |
| Week 3 | Build a small integration/use case |
| Week 4 | Open source contribution (see below) |

### Open Source Contribution Strategy

Contributing to open source in crypto is the fastest way to demonstrate competence. You don't need permission—you need documentation and follow-through.

**Where to find good first issues:**

1. **GMX Protocol GitHub** (gmx-io organization)
   - Look for issues labeled `good-first-issue` or `help-wanted`
   - Start with documentation improvements before tackling core contracts
   - Repo: `gmx-io/gmx-synthetics` (v2), `gmx-io/gmx-contracts`

2. **Uniswap Labs GitHub**
   - `uniswap-org/uniswap-interface` — Frontend issues
   - `uniswap-org/v4-core` — If you're a protocol engineer type

3. **Aave GitHub**
   - `aave/aave-v3-core` — Protocol contracts
   - `aave/interface` — Frontend

**How to contribute (without buying tokens):**

1. **Fork the repo**
2. **Find a documented issue** — Comment "I'm working on this" so others know
3. **Submit a PR with clear documentation** — Explain what you changed and why
4. **Engage on GitHub discussions** — Answer questions, clarify documentation

**This is your portfolio.** When you apply to GMX, you can say "I contributed to gmx-synthetics issue #X" and link the PR. That carries weight.

### Networking That Doesn't Feel Like Networking

**The "Value-First" Outreach Method:**

1. **Find someone doing interesting work** — A builder on a protocol you follow
2. **Consume their content** — Read their GitHub, their tweets, their governance posts
3. **Add genuine value** — "I noticed your proposal mentioned X—here's a similar pattern from Y protocol that might be useful"
4. **Ask a specific question** — Not "are you hiring" but "I'm interested in your execution model for liquidations—did you consider X approach?"
5. **Let it breathe** — One interaction doesn't obligate a response

**LinkedIn Cold Outreach Template:**

> Hi [Name], I noticed your work on [specific thing]. I'm currently exploring [specific role type] in the DeFi space and found your approach to [specific topic] interesting because [genuine specific reason]. Would you have 15 minutes to chat about [specific question]? I'm not asking for a job—just advice.

**The key:** Make it about the work, not the job. People hire people they like and respect. Start by building respect.

## 3. Community Participation Playbook

### Engaging in GMX Governance (No Token Required)

You can participate in GMX governance without holding GMX tokens through the **GMC (GMX Governance Committee)** process and on-chain governance interactions.

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Read the governance docs** — [docs.gmx.io](https://docs.gmx.io) has a governance section
2. **Join the GMX Discord** → `#governance` channel
3. **Follow active proposals** — Look for GMC proposals on the [governance repository](https://github.com/gmx-io/gmx-governance)
4. **Engage thoughtfully:**
   - Post in governance channels with analysis (not just opinions)
   - Example: "This proposal changes the fee structure. Looking at v1 data, similar changes on Uniswap resulted in [X]. Worth considering [Y] as a safeguard"
5. **Participate in temperature checks** — These are informal signaling mechanisms

**Credible contribution ideas:**
- Risk analysis of proposals
- Data analysis of protocol metrics
- UX feedback on protocol interfaces
- Documentation improvements
- Translation contributions (if multilingual)

### Developer Discord Best Practices

Discord can be overwhelming. Here's how to engage without drowning:

**Channel hierarchy:**
- `#announcements` — Read-only, always
- `#rules` — Read once
- `#general` — Noise, mostly
- `#development` or `#dev` — High value, read daily
- `#governance` — If you're into that
- Project-specific channels (e.g., `#gmx-synthetics`) — Join your focus areas

**The "Search First, Ask Second" rule:**
Before asking anything:
1. Search the Discord for the topic: `in:#development @someone question about X`
2. Check the channel topic/pinned messages
3. Check if there's a docs link in the channel header

**How to ask good questions:**

**Bad:** "How do I build on GMX?"
**Good:** "I'm trying to implement [specific feature] using [specific method from docs]. I got [specific error/result] when I tried [specific approach]. Has anyone tackled this pattern before?"

**Better (if you have context):** "In the gmx-synthetics repo, the `createOrder` function requires [X]. For a long-position order, is [Y] the correct parameterization, or should I be looking at [Z] based on the v2 pattern?"

**Key elements of a good question:**
- What you're trying to accomplish
- What you've already tried
- What specific result you got
- Relevant links/code snippets

### What NOT to Do (Reputation Destroyers)

These will get you banned, mocked, or quietly excluded:

**🚫 Don't:**
- DM people asking for jobs without prior engagement
- Spam the same question across multiple channels
- Ask questions answered in pinned messages
- "Did anyone see the price of [token]?" — Not relevant in dev channels
- Promote your own token/project in dev Discords
- Argue in bad faith about tokenomics in governance channels
- Be rude to community moderators
- Dump speculative opinions as facts
- Re-post someone else's work claiming it as your own
- Engage with people you disagree with by questioning their motives

**🚫 In job applications:**
- Mass-apply with generic cover letters
- List "crypto enthusiasm" as your only qualification
- Claim expertise you don't have
- Ghost interviewers after scheduling calls

**Positive alternative:** If you want to stand out, do the work. Build something. Write an analysis. Submit a meaningful PR. Then you have something concrete to point to.

## 4. Technical Keep-Up Routine

### Staying Current with EIP Changes

Ethereum Improvement Proposals (EIPs) shape the protocols you'll build on. Here's how to track them without spending your whole life reading:

**Daily/Weekly Tracking:**

1. **EIP status page** — [eips.ethereum.org](https://eips.ethereum.org)
   - Check "Last Call" and "Review" stage EIPs weekly
   - "Draft" EIPs are early—skip unless you're specializing in that area

2. **Ethereum Foundation blog** — [blog.ethereum.org](https://blog.ethereum.org)
   - All-hands announcements for major EIPs

3. **AllCoreDevs calls** — Listen to these for technical direction:
   - Notes posted on [github.com/ethereum/pm](https://github.com/ethereum/pm)
   - Join the Eth R&D Discord for call announcements

4. **Twitter follows for EIPs:**
   - `@lightclient` — EF researchers
   - `@ VitalikButerin` — for high-signal links to proposals
   - `@ethereumcatherders` — EIP status tracking

**What to actually read:**

Focus on EIPs that are in "Last Call" or "Final" stage. The rest is noise for a job hunter unless you're specifically targeting EVM protocol development.

**Current high-signal EIPs to know:**
- EIP-4844 (Proto-Danksharding) — What it does, why it matters
- EIP-1153 (Transient Storage) — Opcode change for developers
- EIP-5656 (MCOPY) — Memory copying opcodes
- EVRM (Ethereum VM in Rust) — If you're a Rust/Solidity dev

### Understanding New DeFi Primitives

New primitives appear regularly. Here's how to build understanding systematically:

**The 3-Layer Understanding Method:**

1. **What it does** (user-level) — Use the protocol. Actually use it (testnet if mainnet costs money). Understand the UX.

2. **How it works** (mechanism-level) — Read the whitepaper or documentation. Look at the contract code. Understand the state transitions.

3. **Why it was designed this way** (tradeoffs) — Compare to similar protocols. Read governance discussions about design decisions.

**High-value primitives to understand right now:**

| Primitive | Example Protocols | Key Concept |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| **AMM** | Uniswap, Curve | x*y=k, concentrated liquidity |
| **Lending** | Aave, Compound | Pool-based lending, health factors |
| **CDP** | MakerDAO | Collateralized debt positions |
| **Derivatives** | GMX, dYdX, Polymarket | Perpetual futures, prediction markets |
| **Restaking** | EigenLayer | Restaking ETH for economic security |
| **Liquid Staking** | Lido, Rocket Pool | Staking derivatives |
| **Hooks** | Uniswap v4 | Extensibility via lifecycle hooks |

**For each primitive you target for employment:**
- Build a small demo (even a testnet version)
- Write a 1-page analysis of the design tradeoffs
- Find one governance proposal that changed it and understand why

| **Follow-up Date** | Date | When to follow up |
| **Notes** | Long text | Any relevant context, questions asked, your answers |
| **Portfolio Link** | URL | Link to relevant work you can share |

### The Tracker Workflow

**Daily (1 min):** Update any status changes
**After each interview (10 min):** Write down questions asked, your answers, what felt strong/weak
**Weekly (15 min):** Review "Wishlist" items, move any to "Applied" if posting appears

### Finding Warm Introductions

Warm introductions are 10x more effective than cold applications. Here's how:

**Method 1: The Two-Handshake Rule**
1. Find someone at your target company (e.g., a GMX engineer)
2. Look at who they interact with on Twitter/Discord
3. See if you share any mutual connections
4. Ask the mutual for an introduction with context ("I see you know X who works on Y—I'm interested in Z and would love a 15-min intro")

**Method 2: Event Networking**
- ETHGlobal hackathons — Build something, meet team members
- DAO governance calls — Introduce yourself in chat after meaningful contributions
- Twitter spaces — Participate, share insights, follow up

**Method 3: Content Bridge**
1. Write something substantive (analysis, tutorial, documentation improvement)
2. Tag or mention the people it would interest
3. Natural conversation follows

**Email template for warm intro request:**

> Hi [Mutual Connection],
>
> I've been following your work on [specific project] and I'm interested in breaking into [specific role type] in the DeFi space.
>
> I noticed you know [Target Person] through [context]. Would you feel comfortable making a brief intro? I'm not asking for a job—just 15 minutes of their time to learn about their work.
>
> I've been [specific relevant activity] and think [Target Person]'s team would be a strong fit for my background.
>
> Happy to return the favor anytime with any connections or insights I might have.

## 6. Mental Health & Burnout Prevention

### Why This Matters

Crypto has a documented culture of overwork, 24/7 markets, and "sleep is for the weak" mentality. This is both stupid and counterproductive. You will make better decisions, write better code, and interview better when you're rested.

### The 24/7 Problem

Markets never sleep, governance happens at all hours, and there's always more to read. You must actively create boundaries.

**Concrete Boundaries:**

1. **No crypto news before 9am** — Protect your morning
2. **No Discord after 8pm** — Batch check in the morning
3. **One full day off per week** — Saturday or Sunday, no exceptions
4. **No Twitter on weekends** — Or limit to 10 minutes
5. **Notification off on phone** — All day, every day (crypto apps especially)

### The "Good Enough" Standard

You cannot read everything. You will never be fully caught up. Accept this.

**Information consumption framework:**
- Do I need to know this **today**? (Yes → read now)
- Is it interesting but not urgent? (Yes → bookmark for weekend deep dive)
- Is it neither urgent nor interesting? (Yes → ignore)

### When to Take Breaks

**Warning signs you're approaching burnout:**
- Reading the same tweet 3 times because you can't focus
- Checking prices/notifications as a stress response, not interest
- Dreading opening Discord or Twitter
- Making careless errors in code or communication
- Feeling persistently irritable about the space

**Mandatory break triggers:**
- If you catch yourself doomscrolling for 30+ minutes: Put the phone down
- If you're reading crypto news at midnight: Close the laptop
- If you're checking prices more than 3x/day: Set hard limits
- If a protocol "drama" has you stressed: Step away until it's resolved (it will be)

### Sustainable Routine Template

**Daily:**
| Time | Activity | Duration | Notes |
|------|----------|----------|-------|
| Morning | News scan (newsletter previews) | 15 min | Skim only |
| Mid-morning | Twitter list check | 10 min | Timer on |
| Afternoon | Discord (if needed for job) | 10 min | Specific channels only |
| Evening | None | — | Protect evenings |

**Weekly:**
| Day | Activity | Duration |
|-----|----------|----------|
| Saturday | Deep dive (one topic) | 90 min |
| Saturday | Job applications | 30 min |
| Sunday | Job opening scan | 30 min |
| Sunday | Rest | Full day |

**The key insight:** This routine is about sustainable intensity, not constant presence. You cannot out-hustle the market. You can out-prepare it.
